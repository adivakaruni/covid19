Mayday On Ground: Did Air Travel Help Spread Coronavirus?

Anantha Divakaruni
Dept. of Economics, University of Bergen, Norway
anantha.divakaruni@uib.no

April 12, 2020

Introduction

The world is waging an uphill battle against Coronavirus (COVID-19). With no cure in sight and a surge of infections worldwide, governments have had little choice but to impose unprecedented restrictions on social and economic activity. Although the COVID-19 pandemic originated in December 2019 in Wuhan, China, data from various sources show that the disease spread rapidly to over 184 countries and regions in just under four months.1 2 3 Among these, the United States (US) has been the worst affected with over 522,000 cases and 20,000 deaths at the time of this writing. While this in itself is alarming, the rapidness with which the pandemic spread throughout the country is even more striking. There were just 7 confirmed cases across 6 US counties by end January, with most having returned from China recently. February also saw just 70 new cases across 22 counties, raising hopes that the US might have evaded the pandemic. These hopes were dashed in the coming weeks as over 188,000 new cases were reported across 2,154 counties in March 2020.

How did COVID-19 spread so quickly within the US? What factors have led to its rapid spread across the country? In this article, I explore whether air travel might have contributed to the pandemic's rapid spread due to small world network effects. The massive growth in air travel in recent decades means that an infectious disease like COVID-19 can spread very quickly between regions. This can be further aggravated by urbanisation, with densely populated areas such as cities experiencing high rates of infection and also becoming major transmission nodes in the disease diffusion network. Such rapid transmission was detected during the severe acute respiratory syndrome (SARS) outbreak of 2003, which began in Hong Kong and spread rapidly as infected people travelled to other countries (Hollingsworth et al., 2007).

Air travel in the United States

The United States boasts an extensive air transportation network. According to 2019 data from the Bureau of Transportation Statistics (BTS), there were 1,272 airports with scheduled passenger services, of which 37 airports handled over 10 million passengers annually. In addition, the 50 busiest airports handled 82% of all traffic in the country. Due to increased competition, air travel has become more affordable and is the preferred means of transportation, especially for domestic destinations.4 Figure 1 shows the connectivity between various airports in 2019.

Figure 1 shows there is high connectivity between major cities and hubs (e.g. New York–Chicago) that require short flights of up to 2 hours. There is also considerable travel between regions much further away from each other (e.g. New York–Los Angeles) requiring longer flights of up to 6 hours. Clearly, distance does not seem to be a problem as flying is now cheaper and more frequent. Figure 2 plots the density of incoming air travellers per county based on 2019 data from the BTS. While major urban hubs clearly lead the pack, Figure 2 shows that other counties (including rural ones) also experienced a large share of passenger inflows. These patterns highlight two important aspects: (i) Counties that are large population centers naturally attract more travelers, (ii) There is high spatial mobility across counties.

These high levels of connectivity indicate greater mobility among people, and have been shown to directly affect urbanisation and economc activity (GDP). However, connectivity can become a huge problem in the event of a pandemic. Places visited more frequently (such as tourist hubs) will experience more introductions of the infection, especially if incoming passengers are not screened thoroughly at airports. Infected travelers will unknowingly infect others through person-to-person (P2P) transmission, causing a greater spatial spread of the pathogen. Despite this important connection between mobility and infectious disease spread, it has been overlooked by most epidemiological studies to date.

Modelling the spread of COVID-19

I use a variation of the classical SIR model described in Wesolowski et al. (2017) to model the spread of COVID-19 in the United States. Conditional on infected persons traveling into the US, the transmission of the virus to other places depends on the locations of initial infections and their connectivity to the rest of the country.

Let $N_i \in [1, N]$ represent the population of county $i$. At any time $t$, individuals in the population are classified as being either Susceptible to the disease ($S_{i,t}$), currently Infected and capable of transmitting the disease to susceptible individuals ($I_{i,t}$), and, Recovered or dead from the infection ($R_{i,t}$). The total population $N_{i,t} = S_{i,t} + I_{i,t} + R_{i,t}$. All individuals are in susceptible group $S$ prior to the onset of the pandemic. Individuals in $S$ that come into contact with an infected individual and get infected are moved into the infectious group $I$. Infecteds that get cured or become immune move to the recovered group $R$.

In each county, an outbreak will occur if there is contact with an infected person who is already present (or) coming in from elsewhere. Both the transmission rate and the susceptible population size will determine the intensity of the outbreak. The time-varying probability (hazard) of an outbreak occurring at county $j$ can be expressed as:5 $$ h(t,j) = \frac{\beta S_{j,t} (1-\exp(-\sum_{k} m_{j,k}^t x_{k,t} y_{j,t}))}{1 + \beta {y_{j,t}}} \tag{1} $$ where $\beta$ is the disease's transmission rate; $S_{j,t}$ is the proportion of population in county $j$ at time $t$ that is susceptible; $m_{j,k}^t$ denotes mobility from county $k$ to county $j$; $x_{k,t}$ and $y_{j,t}$ represent the proportion of infected and susceptible populations on day $t$ at counties $k$ and $j$, respectively, and can be estimated as $x_{k,t} = \frac{I_{k,t}}{N_k}$ and $y_{j,t} = \frac{S_{j,t}}{N_j}$ ($N_k$ and $N_j$ are the total populations (normalized) at counties $k$ and $j$, respectively). The transmission rate $\beta$ of the COVID-19 pathogen, and is assumed to not vary spatially. I then simulate the stochastic process of the disease being introduced into counties as a Bernoulli random variable $ I_{j, t+1} \sim Binom(h(t, j)) $ for $ I_t = 0 $. (Note: In unreported simulations, I use a more deterministic approach and introduce the disease initially into the 6 counties that actually reported COVID-19 cases in January 2020. The results are strikingly similar to the simulations presented below.)

Upon being introduced into initial counties, the virus is transmitted by infected individuals travelling to other locations. The mechanism through which the disease is carried can be proxied using an inflow-outflow (IO) matrix of the number of passengers travelling by air between counties.

Another important parameter required for the simulation is the basic reproduction number $R_0$, defined as the expected number of infections that can occur upon the introduction of an infected individual into a susceptible population. $ R_0 = s_0 \frac{\beta}{\gamma} $, where $s_0$ is the initial proportion of susceptibles and $\gamma$ the rate of recovery or mortality (expressed as $\frac{1}{\tau}$, where $\tau$ is the expected duration of infection in days).

For the purposes of simulation, I assume it takes up to 20 days for an infected patient to fully recover (or die). Thus, $\gamma = 0.04$. Moreover, many airlines have been forced to limit or suspend operations due to reduced demand as several states and counties have imposed stay-at-home (SAH) and shelter-in-place (SIP) restrictions.

The fractions of the population $S$, $I$, and $R$ evolve over time as follows:5 $$ S_{j,t+1} = S_{j,t} - \frac{\beta S_{j,t}I_{j,t}}{N_j} - \frac{\alpha \beta S_{j,t} \sum_{k} m_{j,k}^t x_{k,t}}{N_j + \sum_{k} m_{j,k}^t} \tag{2.1} $$ $$ I_{j,t+1} = I_{j,t} + \frac{\beta S_{j,t}I_{j,t}}{N_j} + \frac{\alpha \beta S_{j,t} \sum_{k} m_{j,k}^t x_{k,t}}{N_j + \sum_{k} m_{j,k}^t} - \gamma I_{j,t} \tag{2.2} $$ $$ R_{j,t+1} = R_{j,t} + \gamma I_{j,t} \tag{2.3} $$ where $\alpha$ is a parameter denoting the percentage of people travelling by air versus other modes of transportation. Equation $2.1$ shows that the susceptible population $S_{j,t}$ in county $j$ on day $t + 1$ reduces from the previous day by the fraction of new infections and the fraction of infected people flying from other counties into $j$ (In more recent analysis, I include an additional term for the fraction of infected people leaving the county. More on this later!). These new infections are added back in equation $2.2$ to reflect the new infected population in county $j$ on day $t + 1$. The additional term $\gamma I_{j,t}$ is because infected people either recover or die at the rate $\gamma$, and consequently leave the infected group. This term is moved to equation $2.3$ so that the total population $N_j = S_j + I_j + R_j$ remains constant.

Since social restrictions are likely to affect air travel, the simulations are performed for two different scenarios:
  1. No social restrictions: implies that people who prefer to travel by air will not change their decision even after learning about the pandemic. Here, I set $\alpha = 1$.
  2. Social distancing: with social restrictions in place, flight connectivity is likely to drop and many people are expected to refrain from travel. Hence, I set $\alpha = 0.5$ if county $k$ has SAH or SIP restrictions in place on day $t$. I also modify the IO matrix according to the social restrictions in place across counties during each day.

Simulations: Spread of COVID-19

The first set of simulations considers the scenario where there are no mandatory social restrictions in place and that people who prefer to travel will continue to do so (i.e., $\alpha = 1$). In the second set of simulations, I assess the impact of a drop in flight connectivity due to the imposition of social restrictions at various counties (i.e., $\alpha = 0.5$). Data on SAH and SIP orders issued by individual states and counties was obtained from the New York Times.
Figure 3 plots fractions of the total US population that is susceptible ($S$), with an active infection ($I$), or that has recovered or died ($R$) during the first 100 days under the first scenario where $\alpha = 1$. The virus spreads quickly and infects up to 50% of the population by day 24. Naturally, the health system is quite easily overwhelmed at this point as it does not have a capacity (in terms of ICU beds and ventilators) beyond 1% (Atkeson, 2020). Thereafter, the disease recedes very slowly and does not go away completely even after day 100, possibly due to residual person-to-person transmission and the onset of warmer temperatures. This raises the chances that the pandemic may return the following winter if no social distancing policies are in place.

Figure 4 shows SIR estimates under the actual ongoing situation where air travel has been largely curtailed due to reduced demand and the drastic measures taken to reduce social contact (i.e., $\alpha = 0.5$). The estimates suggest that the infection will peak much later around day 40 (compared to day 24 in the previous scenario) and will infect a significantly lower fraction of the population (around 36% compared to 50% in the preceding scenario). The disease recedes gradually over the following days, but does not go away entirely from the population. Nevertheless, it is easy to see that reduced air travel can have a big impact on mitigating the spatial diffusion of the disease, providing a much needed breathing space for the health system to cope up.
To gain better perspective, I use the SIR model estimates to understand the evolution of infections across counties under the two scenarios. Figure 5 shows a visual animation of the proportion of infected persons per county over the 100-day period under the first scenario (no social restrictions). It is evident that with greater mobility the infection spreads in a short span of time to several counties. The spatial dynamics of the virus are no longer restricted to major population centers, and can spread quickly across both urban and rural counties. Interestingly, popular tourist destinations such as Yellowstone National Park and the Colorado Ski country will also become virus hotspots, helping spread the disease rapidly once vacationers return to their home counties.
Figure 6 shows COVID-19 dynamics across US counties under reduced air connectivity (second scenario). Infections are much lower in most counties with some notable exceptions like big cities and tourist destinations. This provides clear evidence that although curbing air travel will minimize the geographic spread of COVID-19, strict social distancing (to minimize person-to-person contact) should also be enacted early on to contain this menace.
